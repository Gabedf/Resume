Engineering perspective

How I Think About Software

A collection of ideas, principles, and mental models that shape how I approach engineering problems. Not rigid rules — just patterns of thinking I've found consistently useful.

01

Simplicity in Systems

There is a constant temptation in software to add — more abstraction, more layers, more configuration options. I try to resist it. The most resilient systems tend to be the ones where every component earns its place. Complexity should be a deliberate choice made under constraint, not a default side effect of building fast. When I review my own code, the first question I ask is: what can I remove without breaking the intent?

02

Systems Over Features

It's relatively easy to ship a feature. It's much harder — and more valuable — to build the system that makes the next ten features easy to ship. I care about both, but I believe good engineers spend a disproportionate amount of energy thinking about architecture relative to any individual feature. A well-designed system is a force multiplier; a poorly-designed one compounds its cost with every change.

03

Data and Decision Making

Data doesn't make decisions — people do. But engineers who understand the data flowing through their systems tend to make better decisions than those who don't. Whether it's query performance, error rates, or user behaviour patterns, I try to stay close to the numbers. Not because data is always decisive, but because it grounds reasoning in reality and keeps intuition honest.

04

Learning by Building

Most of what I know, I learned by doing something that didn't work the first time. Documentation teaches you syntax. Shipping broken code teaches you systems. I seek out projects that are just slightly beyond my current ability — things that require me to figure out new failure modes, not just apply existing patterns. The poker simulator I built taught me more about probability than any statistics course ever did. The discomfort of not knowing is usually where the real learning starts.

05

The Engineering Mindset

Good engineering is as much about asking questions as it is about writing code. What problem are we actually solving? What are the failure modes? Who maintains this in six months? I've found that slowing down at the start of a problem — really interrogating the requirements before touching the keyboard — consistently leads to better outcomes than jumping straight to implementation. The best code I've written started with a long pause.
